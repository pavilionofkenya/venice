Curatorial Statement

“Exercises in Conversation” explores the relationships and dynamics between participants in a conversation, the inter-changing roles of both talker and listener, and how this complex relationship affects, influences and occupies the space of stories, of history and of pedagogy.

Both as a conceptual basis and as a curatorial approach, the project assumes a philosophical and methodological stance, proposing a key role in understanding the actor and the subject regarding a particular event or phenomena relating to both their lives, and the dialectics between engagement, on the one hand, and autonomy, on the other.

Aiming to further conversations and ongoing debates about the global and the local, language and translation, originality and mimicry, the personal and the collective, silence and noise, this presentation invites conversations devoid of authoritative moments that do not generate authoritative claims. Through their work, the artists prompt an understanding of consciousness that is developed through embodied experience.

The individual practices of the four artists representing the Pavilion of Kenya are strongly rooted in their respective identities and stories, while approaching their art-making through a distinguished use of language, meaning and forms, pushing conceptual and material boundaries, and in the process, responding to the kaleidoscopic nature in which Kenyan contemporaneity continues to be conceived.

It considers all consciousness as intentional – in other words when we try to make sense of something it is always with a purpose in mind. We have to supply the purpose and this leads to the existentialist viewpoint which posits that there is no a priori meaning to existence, the world just is and that we have to face up to this existential circumstance.

As a phenomenological framework, “Exercises in Conversation” proposes new relationships between reality as a construct and the imaginary as a possibility. As curator Cecilia Alemani suggests, the 59th International Art exhibition asks artists not to reveal who we are, but to absorb the worries and fears of this time and show us who and what we can become.

JIMMY OGONGA

Jimmy Ogonga is an artist and producer, working intermittently between art production and curatorial practice. In 2001, he founded a polyvalent space, the Centre for Contemporary Art, East Africa (CCA, Nairobi), an initiative that worked as a catalyst for the visual arts and the creation of significant art projects.

From 2006 to 09, he curated Amnesia, a multidisciplinary roving research and exhibition project that brought together artists from Africa and its scattered Diasporas in a series of production, exhibition & conversation platforms in several countries.

He has co-curated projects including Space: Currencies in Contemporary African Art (2010, Johannesburg), and was a member of “Incubator for a Pan-African roaming biennial,” a yearlong project arranged to facilitate the articulation of critical positions regarding the notion of a Pan-African Biennial, as part of Manifesta 8 in Spain.

He was a technical advisor (2013) and is co-curator (2014) of the Addis Ababa Foto Festival, as well as a current faculty member for the Livingston Office for Contemporary Art, Livingstone, Zambia.

Ogonga was Curator of the Kenya Pavilion for the 57th Venice Biennale, is an alumnus of the Rijksakademie, Amsterdam and is currently the director of Kurabu Projects.

ABOUT KURABU PROJECTS

Kurabu Projects is an itinerant practice-focused observatory for art, culture & society based in Malindi, Nairobi and Kisumu, Kenya. Kurabu Projects works collaboratively with a range of practitioners to develop significant multi-disciplinary projects that serve as catalysts for culture, knowledge and social transformation.
